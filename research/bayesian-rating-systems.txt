Comparative Analysis of Bayesian State-Space Rating Systems and Continuous Performance State EstimationThe statistical evaluation of competitor ability has evolved from static, heuristic point-estimate systems into a sophisticated branch of Bayesian state-space modeling. Classic methodologies, such as the Elo rating system, assume that a competitor’s skill is a fixed, deterministic quantity. Modern frameworks, however, treat skill as a dynamic, unobserved latent state that must be inferred from sequential, noisy observations. This analysis evaluates the mathematical formulations of the Glicko, Glicko-2, TrueSkill, and TrueSkill Through Time (TTT) rating systems, detailing how each represents latent skill, accumulates uncertainty over time, and models skill volatility. Finally, this analysis contrasts these ordinal pairwise comparison systems with a continuous-performance state-space model—specifically, a log-solve-time Kalman filter—demonstrating the structural, analytical, and computational advantages of moving from non-Gaussian approximations to exact linear-Gaussian state estimation.Latent Skill and Uncertainty RepresentationBayesian rating systems model competitor skill as a latent random variable rather than a fixed scalar, maintaining a continuous probability distribution over a player's true ability. Match results serve as noisy evidence used to update this distribution via Bayes' theorem.Glicko and Glicko-2 RepresentationsThe original Glicko system models a competitor's skill using a Gaussian belief distribution characterized by a rating $r$ (the mean of the belief distribution) and a ratings deviation $RD$ (the standard deviation, representing the model's uncertainty). A competitor's true skill is assumed to lie within the 95% confidence interval of $$.Glicko-2 maintains this fundamental Gaussian state but scale-transforms these parameters onto a canonical scale to simplify the mathematical updates. Given a player's rating $r$ and deviation $RD$, Glicko-2 maps them to the canonical latent skill mean $\mu$ and canonical uncertainty $\phi$ :$$\mu = \frac{r - 1500}{173.7178}$$$$\phi = \frac{RD}{173.7178}$$The canonical latent skill distribution is represented as $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \phi^2)$. Glicko-2 introduces a third parameter, the rating volatility $\sigma$, which represents the expected standard deviation of the competitor's latent skill changes over a rating period. Volatility is maintained on its original scale and governs the adaptive rate of the state transition.TrueSkill RepresentationThe TrueSkill system represents a player's latent skill $s_i$ as a univariate Gaussian distribution :$$p(s_i) = \mathcal{N}(s_i; \mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$$where $\mu_i$ represents the estimated mean ability, and $\sigma_i^2$ represents the variance or system uncertainty. TrueSkill assumes that a player’s performance $p_i$ in any given match is a noisy sample drawn around their latent skill :$$p(p_i \mid s_i) = \mathcal{N}(p_i; s_i, \beta^2)$$where $\beta^2$ represents the performance variance, modeling game-specific noise, luck, or day-to-day performance fluctuation. Because displaying a high mean rating with high uncertainty could mislead leaderboard standings, practical deployments of TrueSkill often display a conservative skill estimate. This conservative rating is typically defined as the 1% lower quantile of the belief distribution :$$\text{Display Rating} = \mu_i - 3\sigma_i$$This ensures that top positions on a leaderboard are reserved for players who are both highly skilled and estimated with high certainty.TrueSkill Through Time RepresentationTrueSkill Through Time (TTT) extends TrueSkill from a sequential filter to a global smoothing model. TTT represents a competitor's skill over a sequence of time periods $t \in \{1, \dots, T\}$ as a trajectory of latent variables $(s^1_i, s^2_i, \dots, s^T_i)$. The prior belief over the player's initial skill is a factorizing Gaussian :$$p(s^0_i) = \mathcal{N}(s^0_i; \mu_0, \sigma^2_0)$$Each latent skill variable $s^t_i$ is coupled with the skill variables of the adjacent time steps $s^{t-1}_i$ and $s^{t+1}_i$ through dynamic transition factors. Within any period $t$, a player's match performance $p^t_{ij}$ is drawn around their current latent skill $s^t_i$ :$$p(p^t_{ij} \mid s^t_i) = \mathcal{N}(p^t_{ij}; s^t_i, \beta^2)$$This structure allows information from any point in the player's career to propagate forward and backward, refining the entire estimated trajectory.Temporal Dynamics and Uncertainty Inflation during InactivityA robust state-space rating model must account for the degradation of information when a competitor is inactive. Without matches to observe, the system’s uncertainty regarding the competitor's true skill must inflate over time.Glicko and Glicko-2 Inflation MechanicsIn the original Glicko system, the Ratings Deviation ($RD$) increases based on the elapsed time $t$ (measured in rating periods) since the competitor's last match :$$RD' = \min\left(\sqrt{RD^2 + c^2 t}, 350\right)$$where $c$ is a constant governing the rate of uncertainty accumulation, and $350$ represents the $RD$ of an unrated player. The constant $c$ is derived from the amount of time required for a player's rating deviation to grow back to that of an unrated player. For example, if it is assumed that it takes 100 rating periods for a typical player's $RD$ to grow from an active baseline of $50$ back to the initial uncertainty of $350$, the constant $c$ is computed by solving :$$350 = \sqrt{50^2 + c^2 (100)} \implies c = \sqrt{\frac{350^2 - 50^2}{100}} \approx 34.64$$In Glicko-2, if a player is inactive for a rating period, their canonical rating $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$ remain unchanged, while their canonical rating deviation $\phi$ inflates based on their current volatility :$$\phi^* = \sqrt{\phi^2 + \sigma^2}$$If a player remains inactive across multiple rating periods, this additive volatility step compounds, raising the latent uncertainty toward the unrated limit.TrueSkill and TTT Inflation MechanicsTrueSkill and TTT model skill changes over time using a discrete-time random walk state transition. Between consecutive active periods or time steps, a Gaussian transition factor is introduced :$$p(s^t_i \mid s^{t-1}_i) = \mathcal{N}(s^t_i; s^{t-1}_i, \tau^2)$$where $\tau^2$ (sometimes denoted as $\gamma^2$) represents the transition noise variance, or the rate at which skills are allowed to drift over time. When computing the prior for the next match sequentially, this transition increases the variance of the belief distribution :$$\sigma_{t \mid t-1}^2 = \sigma_{t-1}^2 + \tau^2$$In classic online TrueSkill, this uncertainty inflation is purely forward-looking, accumulating variance indefinitely during periods of inactivity. In TrueSkill Through Time, however, this inflation is retrospectively bounded: when an inactive player returns and plays matches, the backward message-passing pass uses those subsequent performances to constrain and reduce the uncertainty along the historical inactive period.The Mechanics of Separate Volatility StatesCompetitors do not learn or degrade at a uniform rate. Some experience rapid changes in skill due to training or physical factors, while others maintain stable performance levels over long careers. Modeling these varying rates of skill drift requires a separate volatility state.Glicko-2 Volatility UpdatesGlicko-2 features a player-specific, time-varying volatility state $\sigma$. Volatility scales the rate of uncertainty inflation, allowing the rating system to adapt quickly when a player exhibits highly erratic performances (e.g., exceptional wins after a long period of stability).Updating a player's volatility $\sigma$ to $\sigma'$ after a rating period requires solving an optimization problem. Given a player with canonical rating $\mu$ and rating deviation $\phi$, competing against $m$ opponents with parameters $(\mu_1, \phi_1), \dots, (\mu_m, \phi_m)$ and outcomes $s_1, \dots, s_m \in \{1, 0.5, 0\}$ (representing win, draw, loss) :Compute the estimated variance of the player's rating based on game outcomes $v$ :
$$v = \left[ \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\phi_j)^2 E(\mu, \mu_j, \phi_j) \{1 - E(\mu, \mu_j, \phi_j)\} \right]^{-1}$$Compute the rating improvement quantity $\Delta$ :
$$\Delta = v \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\phi_j) \{s_j - E(\mu, \mu_j, \phi_j)\}$$Define the auxiliary function $f(x)$ where $x = \ln(\sigma'^2)$ :
$$f(x) = \frac{e^x (\Delta^2 - \phi^2 - v - e^x)}{2(\phi^2 + v + e^x)^2} - \frac{x - a}{\tau^2}$$
where $a = \ln(\sigma^2)$ and $\tau$ is a system constant constraining the rate of change in volatility over time.Set the initial limits for the numerical root-finding procedure: $A = a = \ln(\sigma^2)$. If $\Delta^2 > \phi^2 + v$, set $B = \ln(\Delta^2 - \phi^2 - v)$. Otherwise, find a constant $k \ge 1$ by decrementing $k$ by $1$ until $f(a - k\tau) < 0$, and set $B = a - k\tau$.Execute the Illinois algorithm—a modified regula falsi procedure—to find the root $C$ such that $f(C) = 0$. In each iteration, compute $C = A + (A - B)\frac{f(A)}{f(B) - f(A)}$ and evaluate $f(C)$. If $f(C)f(B) \le 0$, set $A \leftarrow B$ and $f(A) \leftarrow f(B)$; otherwise, set $f(A) \leftarrow f(A)/2$. Then set $B \leftarrow C$ and $f(B) \leftarrow f(C)$. This continues until $|B - A| \le \epsilon$.Set the updated volatility as $\sigma' = e^{C/2}$.Once $\sigma'$ is determined, the prior rating deviation is updated to $\phi^* = \sqrt{\phi^2 + \sigma'^2}$, after which the new canonical rating $\mu'$ and deviation $\phi'$ are computed :$$\phi' = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{\phi^{*2}} + \frac{1}{v}}}$$$$\mu' = \mu + \phi'^2 \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(\phi_j) \{s_j - E(\mu, \mu_j, \phi_j)\}$$Volatility in TTT and TrueSkill2Classic TrueSkill and TTT do not feature a separate, player-specific volatility state, relying instead on a fixed, globally tuned skill drift parameter $\tau^2$. TrueSkill2 and modern implementations of TTT address varying rates of skill change through automatic parameter estimation over batches of historical matches.Rather than executing a local heuristic update for each competitor, TrueSkill2/TTT uses maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) or expectation-maximization (EM) over a batch of historical data to optimize the global skill transition parameters. This offline optimization estimates the global drift rate $\tau^2$ and performance variance $\beta^2$ to maximize the model's predictive accuracy across the entire network of historical games, preventing the local rating instability that can affect Glicko-2 during highly erratic, small-sample-size streaks.Comparing Ordinal Outcomes with Continuous Performance MeasurementsGlicko, Glicko-2, and TrueSkill are designed for ordinal outcomes, mapping latent skill differences to discrete win, loss, or draw results. While effective for classic games like chess, this ordinal mapping introduces a significant mathematical bottleneck.The Ordinal Bottleneck and Expectation PropagationWhen rating systems consume ordinal outcomes $y \in \{+1, -1, 0\}$, the likelihood function must model the discrete probability of the observed result. In TrueSkill, the latent performance difference $d = p_i - p_j$ is mapped to a game outcome. A win ($y = +1$) requires the performance difference to exceed a draw margin $\epsilon$, while a draw ($y = 0$) requires the absolute performance difference to be within $\epsilon$ :$$y := \begin{cases} 
+1 & \text{if } d > \epsilon \\ 
-1 & \text{if } d < -\epsilon \\ 
0 & \text{if } |d| \le \epsilon 
\end{cases}$$This mapping introduces step-function indicator constraints (e.g., $\mathbb{I}(d > \epsilon)$) into the factor graph, which are highly non-Gaussian. Because the exact joint posterior distribution after applying these step functions is non-Gaussian (often a truncated Gaussian), updating the player ratings sequentially requires an approximation step. TrueSkill and TTT use Expectation Propagation (EP) to approximate these non-Gaussian posterior marginals with Gaussian distributions by matching the first and second moments.For a win update ($y = +1$), let $t = \frac{\mu_W - \mu_L}{c_{ij}}$ and $\alpha = \frac{\epsilon}{c_{ij}}$, where $c_{ij}^2 = 2\beta^2 + \sigma_W^2 + \sigma_L^2$ represents the total variance of the performance difference. The mean and variance updates are computed using the additive correction term $V(t, \alpha)$ and the multiplicative correction term $W(t, \alpha)$ :$$\mu_W \leftarrow \mu_W + \frac{\sigma_W^2}{c_{ij}} V(t, \alpha)$$$$\sigma_W^2 \leftarrow \sigma_W^2 \left$$where :
$$V(t, \alpha) = \frac{\mathcal{N}(t - \alpha)}{\Phi(t - \alpha)}$$$$W(t, \alpha) = V(t, \alpha) \cdot (V(t, \alpha) + t - \alpha)$$This approximation step is computationally intensive and discards the quantitative margin of victory (e.g., whether a speedrunner won a race by milliseconds or several minutes).Continuous Performance Models and Log-Solve-TimesIf a rating system instead observes a direct, continuous performance measurement—such as a solve time, race duration, or score—the measurement model becomes linear-Gaussian. In domains like speedrunning, puzzle-solving, or physical trials, completion times are positive-valued and heavily right-skewed, naturally following a log-normal distribution. Item Response Theory (IRT) demonstrates that latent task difficulty varies linearly with the logarithm of human completion time.By applying a logarithmic transformation to the observed completion time $T_k$ of a task with known difficulty $d_k$, we can formulate a linear-Gaussian measurement equation :$$y_k = \ln(T_k) = d_k - s_k + v_k, \quad v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R)$$where $y_k$ is the observed log solve time, $s_k$ is the competitor's latent skill, $d_k$ is the latent difficulty of the task, and $v_k$ is Gaussian measurement noise with variance $R$. The likelihood function for this observation is exactly Gaussian :$$p(y_k \mid s_k, d_k) = \mathcal{N}(y_k; d_k - s_k, R)$$Because both the prior skill distribution and the likelihood are Gaussian, the true posterior distribution is also exactly Gaussian. The skill update is computed in closed form using standard linear equations, eliminating the need for Expectation Propagation, numerical moment matching, or iterative regula-falsi root-finding.The Power of TrueSkill Through Time: Offline Smoothing and Message PassingClassic rating systems are sequential online filters, updating a competitor's rating forward in time based on the immediate match outcome. This forward-only propagation prevents the model from updating past skill estimates based on future results.The TTT Factor Graph and Forward-Backward Message PassingTrueSkill Through Time (TTT) represents the entire sequence of player skills, performances, and game outcomes as a single global factor graph, processing the entire competitive history offline. To perform global smoothing, TTT utilizes Expectation Propagation to pass messages both forward and backward along the time series of player skills until convergence is reached :1. Inside-Period Message PassingBecause competitors may play multiple matches within a single time period (creating loops in the factor graph), TTT iteratively updates messages over game outcomes within each period to remove dependencies on game ordering. To prevent self-feedback, the message $m_{f \to p}(p)$ from a skill factor to a performance node is computed by dividing out the previous upward message :$$m_{f(p^t_{ij}, s^t_i) \to p^t_{ij}}(p^t_{ij}) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(p^t_{ij}, s^t_i) p(s^t_i) m_{f(p^t_{ij}, s^t_i) \to s^t_i}(s^t_i) ds^t_i$$2. Across-Period Forward and Backward SweepsForward Pass: Identical to standard sequential filtering. It propagates information forward in time from $t=1$ to $T$, saving the forward transition messages $m_{t_{\tau}(s^{t-1}_i, s^t_i) \to s^t_i}(s^t_i)$ that represent the influence of the prior step's skill estimate on the current step.Backward Pass: Once the forward pass is complete, TTT propagates future information backward in time from $t=T$ down to $1$. The backward transition messages are calculated as :
$$m_{t_{\tau}(s^{t-1}_i, s^t_i) \to s^{t-1}_i}(s^{t-1}_i) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} t_{\tau}(s^{t-1}_i, s^t_i) p(s^t_i) m_{t_{\tau}(s^{t-1}_i, s^t_i) \to s^t_i}(s^t_i) ds^t_i$$
These backward messages update the past skill estimates to reflect subsequent achievements.Analytical and Predictive Advantages of TTTGlobally Smoothed Trajectories: By utilizing the entire observation sequence, TTT produces skill estimates with significantly lower uncertainty across the entire time span. The smoothed covariance $P_{t \mid T}$ is always smaller than or equal to the filtered covariance $P_{t \mid t}$, reflecting the additional information gained from future observations.Reliable Initial Skill Estimates: Online filters start new players with high uncertainty, leading to highly volatile early ratings. TTT retrospectively propagates a player's stable, long-term skill back to their initial matches, establishing accurate early-career ratings without requiring a provisional rating period.Historical and Cross-Era Comparability: Online ratings are prone to inflation or deflation and depend heavily on game ordering, making them difficult to compare across long time spans. By propagating information through a single causal network, TTT ensures that rating estimates at distant points in time are calibrated against the same global network, enabling valid historical comparisons (e.g., comparing chess grandmasters across 150 years).Transferring Concepts to a Log-Solve-Time Kalman Filter and RTS SmootherThe core concepts of TrueSkill Through Time—dynamic state transitions, performance extraction, and forward-backward message passing—can be transferred to continuous performance metrics by combining a Kalman filter with a Rauch-Tung-Striebel (RTS) smoother.State-Space Modeling of Log Solve-TimesLet $x_k \in \mathbb{R}$ represent a competitor's latent skill state at time step $k$.Process Model (Temporal Dynamics)The evolution of the latent skill state over time is modeled as a linear Gaussian process, directly matching the state transition model used in TTT :$$x_k = F_k x_{k-1} + w_k, \quad w_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q_k)$$Assuming a random walk where skill drifts but does not follow a directional trend, the transition matrix is $F_k = 1$, and $Q_k = \tau^2$ represents the process noise variance (the rate at which skill is allowed to drift).Observation Model (Performance Measurement)We observe the logarithm of the task completion time, $y_k = \ln(T_k)$, on a task with known latent difficulty $d_k$ :$$y_k = H_k x_k + d_k + v_k, \quad v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_k)$$The observation matrix is $H_k = -1$ (since higher skill reduces solve times), and $R_k$ represents the performance noise variance (analogous to TrueSkill's performance noise $\beta^2$).The Forward Pass: Kalman FilteringThe forward pass runs a standard Kalman filter, computing the filtered mean $\hat{x}_{k \mid k}$ and variance $P_{k \mid k}$ sequentially at each step $k = 1, \dots, T$ using only past and present observations :Prediction Phase$$\hat{x}_{k \mid k-1} = \hat{x}_{k-1 \mid k-1}$$$$P_{k \mid k-1} = P_{k-1 \mid k-1} + Q_k$$Correction PhaseThe innovation residual $\tilde{y}_k$ and residual covariance $S_k$ are calculated as :$$\tilde{y}_k = y_k - (d_k - \hat{x}_{k \mid k-1})$$$$S_k = P_{k \mid k-1} + R_k$$The Kalman gain $K_k$ and updated estimates are then :$$K_k = -P_{k \mid k-1} S_k^{-1}$$$$\hat{x}_{k \mid k} = \hat{x}_{k \mid k-1} + K_k \tilde{y}_k$$$$P_{k \mid k} = (1 + K_k) P_{k \mid k-1}$$Because the observation model is linear and Gaussian, this forward pass computes the exact posterior mean and variance with zero approximation error, eliminating the need for Expectation Propagation or moment matching.The Backward Pass: Rauch-Tung-Striebel SmoothingOnce all observations have been filtered, the RTS smoother processes the trajectory backward from $k = T-1$ down to $1$. The backward update equations are :Compute the smoothing gain $G_k$:$$G_k = P_{k \mid k} (P_{k+1 \mid k})^{-1}$$Update the smoothed state mean $\hat{x}_{k \mid T}$:$$\hat{x}_{k \mid T} = \hat{x}_{k \mid k} + G_k (\hat{x}_{k+1 \mid T} - \hat{x}_{k+1 \mid k})$$Update the smoothed state covariance $P_{k \mid T}$:$$P_{k \mid T} = P_{k \mid k} + G_k (P_{k+1 \mid T} - P_{k+1 \mid k}) G_k^\top$$This backward recursion is the exact linear-Gaussian equivalent of TTT’s backward pass. It corrects the filtered estimates by incorporating future observations, reducing the overall estimation error and producing a globally optimal, consistent trajectory of the latent skill states.Computational and Analytical Performance GainsExact Conjugacy: Because the likelihood is Gaussian, the posterior is exact. The RTS smoother guarantees the mathematically optimal minimum mean-square error (MMSE) state estimate without requiring any approximations.Single-Pass Efficiency: While TTT must iterate over the entire competitive history multiple times to resolve loops and non-Gaussian updates, the Kalman-RTS filter on log solve-times requires only a single forward and single backward pass to achieve global convergence.Linear-Time Complexity: Mathematically, the continuous smoothing problem over $T$ epochs can be formulated as a system of linear equations where the Hessian matrix of the negative log-posterior is symmetric positive-definite (SPD) and block-tridiagonal. Sequential online filters, such as Glicko, Glicko-2, and classic TrueSkill, are computationally efficient and require storing only the current rating state, making them well-suited for live matchmaking databases. However, this online constraint forces the models to discard the retrospective value of future observations. Consequently, sequential ratings are highly sensitive to the order of matches, suffer from provisional rating artifacts early in a competitor's career, and cannot guarantee calibration across long periods of time.TrueSkill Through Time (TTT) and TrueSkill2 resolve these limitations by framing skill estimation as a global offline smoothing problem. By representing the entire competitive history as a unified factor graph, TTT propagates information backward through time. This bidirectional flow of information reduces historical rating uncertainty, establishes accurate early-career skill estimates, and calibrates ratings against a single global scale, enabling valid comparisons of player ability across different eras.When the competitive format permits a continuous performance metric (such as solve times), the system can be further optimized. Applying a logarithmic transformation to solve times establishes a linear-Gaussian measurement equation, mapping the rating system directly to a Kalman filter and Rauch-Tung-Striebel (RTS) smoother. This continuous formulation avoids the non-Gaussian indicators and truncated probability updates that affect pairwise systems, allowing for exact, closed-form Bayesian smoothing in linear time using sparse block-tridiagonal solvers. For professional sports analytics, historical databases, and quantitative trials, the integration of continuous log-performance measurements with backward smoothing algorithms represents the most robust and mathematically optimal approach to latent skill estimation.
