Dual State Estimation: Computational Frameworks for Lightweight Volatility Tracking and Cognitive Learning CurvesIn dynamic state-space modeling, a central objective is to track the unobserved trajectory of a system state from noisy, sequential measurements. While classical Kalman filtering provides optimal estimation under linear-Gaussian assumptions with known, stationary noise parameters, practical applications frequently violate these conditions. In domains such as algorithmic trading, human-machine interaction, and cognitive tracking, the system's underlying uncertainty and the agent's rate of improvement vary dynamically over time. This paper presents a comprehensive mathematical evaluation of dual-filter architectures. First, it surveys lightweight methods for tracking time-varying measurement volatility in small-sample regimes. Second, it evaluates the mathematical formulation of empirical learning curves and their encoding within linear and non-linear state-space frameworks.Lightweight Methods for Estimating Time-Varying VolatilityWhen tracking a noisy time series, a primary filter is typically deployed to estimate the time-varying mean of the process. Concurrently, a secondary estimator is required to track the time-varying variance, which serves as the dynamic measurement covariance ($R_t$) for the primary filter. To maintain online operational efficiency, the secondary volatility estimator must be computationally lightweight. Three main paradigms exist for this task: observation-driven formulations such as GARCH and Exponentially Weighted Moving Averages (EWMA), and parameter-driven state-space formulations such as Harvey-style stochastic volatility.Survey of Volatility EstimatorsHarvey-Style Stochastic VolatilityOriginally proposed by Taylor  and cast into a linear state-space framework by Harvey, Ruiz, and Shephard , the univariate stochastic volatility model represents a noisy residual $e_t$ as a product of a latent volatility process and an independent shock:$$e_t = \epsilon_t \exp\left(\frac{h_t}{2}\right)$$where $\epsilon_t \sim \operatorname{NID}(0, 1)$ represents the measurement innovation, and $h_t = \ln(\sigma_t^2)$ is the unobserved log-volatility. The dynamics of the latent log-volatility are modeled as an autoregressive process :$$h_{t+1} = \mu + \phi(h_t - \mu) + \eta_t, \quad \eta_t \sim \operatorname{NID}(0, \sigma^2_\eta)$$To apply standard Kalman filtering, the non-linear measurement equation is linearized by taking the logarithm of the squared residuals :$$\ln(e_t^2) = h_t + \ln(\epsilon_t^2)$$Because $\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, the squared term $\epsilon_t^2$ follows a chi-squared distribution with one degree of freedom ($\chi^2_1$). The natural logarithm of a $\chi^2_1$ variable is highly skewed and non-Gaussian, with a known mean and variance :$$E[\ln(\epsilon_t^2)] \approx -1.27, \quad \operatorname{Var}[\ln(\epsilon_t^2)] = \frac{\pi^2}{2} \approx 4.93$$By defining a transformed observation $y^\ell_t = \ln(e_t^2) + 1.27$, the system is expressed in a linear state-space form :$$y^\ell_t = h_t + \xi_t, \quad \xi_t \sim \operatorname{IID}(0, 4.93)$$The latent log-volatility $h_t$ can then be estimated recursively using a standard linear Kalman filter. This approach is called Quasi-Maximum Likelihood (QML).Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH)The GARCH(1,1) model is an observation-driven approach where the conditional variance $\sigma_t^2$ is modeled as a deterministic function of past squared innovations and past variances :$$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha e_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2$$Because the next-period volatility is a deterministic function of historical data, the GARCH model is straightforward to estimate via maximum likelihood. However, GARCH models lack a separate stochastic disturbance for the variance equation. This makes them less flexible than stochastic volatility models when tracking sudden, parameter-driven structural breaks or regime changes. Furthermore, GARCH models are highly sensitive to extreme outliers, which can cause persistent upward bias in forecasted volatility.Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA)The EWMA model updates the variance recursively using a decay factor $\lambda$ (typically chosen between $0.90$ and $0.98$):$$\sigma_t^2 = (1-\lambda) e_t^2 + \lambda \sigma_{t-1}^2$$EWMA is highly computationally efficient and requires no complex numerical optimization. However, like GARCH, it is purely reactive and does not maintain a probability distribution over the latent volatility. By utilizing the properties of the exponential family, the expectation and variance of the logarithm of a chi-squared variable $Y$ with $k$ degrees of freedom can be computed analytically using the digamma function $\psi(z) = \frac{d}{dz}\ln \Gamma(z)$ and the trigamma function $\psi'(z) = \frac{d^2}{dz^2}\ln \Gamma(z)$ :$$E = \ln(2) + \psi\left(\frac{k}{2}\right), \quad \operatorname{Var} = \psi'\left(\frac{k}{2}\right)$$Substituting $k = n-1$, the expectation of the natural logarithm of the sample variance is:$$E[\ln(s_t^2)] = h_t + \ln(2) + \psi\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right) - \ln(n-1)$$To construct a completely unbiased estimator for the latent log-volatility $h_t$, a bias-correction constant $C_n$ must be subtracted from the logged sample variance :$$C_n = \ln(2) + \psi\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right) - \ln(n-1)$$Defining the corrected measurement as $y^\ell_t = \ln(s_t^2) - C_n$ yields an unbiased measurement equation for the secondary filter :$$y^\ell_t = h_t + \xi_t, \quad \xi_t \sim \operatorname{IID}(0, R^\ell_t)$$Because the error term $\xi_t = \ln(Y) - E$ represents the fluctuations of the log-chi-squared variable, its variance is independent of the underlying state $h_t$. This acts as a variance-stabilizing transformation. Analysis of Feedback Loop InstabilityThis cascaded configuration creates a closed-loop system where the state of each filter depends on the output of the other. If this feedback loop is left unconstrained, it can become highly unstable due to a positive feedback mechanism:Covariance Collapse: A sequence of small measurement innovations in the primary filter reduces the local sample variance $s_t^2$.The secondary filter interprets this as a decline in system volatility, updating the log-volatility state $\hat{h}_t$ downward.A lower $\hat{h}_{t\mid t-1}$ decreases the primary filter’s measurement covariance $R_t$.This smaller $R_t$ increases the primary filter's Kalman gain $K_t = P_{t\mid t-1} C^T (C P_{t\mid t-1} C^T + R_t)^{-1}$.With an elevated Kalman gain, the primary filter places more weight on recent measurements, pulling its posterior estimate $\hat{x}_t$ closer to the observations and artificially shrinking subsequent residuals.This artificially deflates the next sample variance $s_{t+1}^2$, driving the volatility estimate $\hat{h}$ and the primary covariance $R_t$ to collapse toward zero. Once collapsed, the primary filter overfits to measurement noise and ignores its process model.Conversely, a large transient outlier can initiate a self-reinforcing upward spiral. A large innovation spike inflates the estimated volatility, which increases $R_t$ and reduces the primary filter's Kalman gain. This causes the primary filter to ignore subsequent measurements, resulting in persistently large residuals that drive the estimated volatility to expand exponentially.Stabilization Engineering SolutionsTo prevent covariance collapse and exponential divergence, the coupled estimator must be stabilized using three concrete architectural constraints:Dynamic Decoupling via One-Step Delay: The primary filter must only use the a priori predicted volatility state $\hat{h}_{t\mid t-1}$ rather than the a posteriori filtered state $\hat{h}_{t\mid t}$ to determine $R_t$. This delay breaks the contemporaneous circularity in the update step, mitigating algebraic loops and stabilizing real-time simulation speeds.Process Noise Floor Constraints: A strict lower bound must be maintained on the secondary filter's process noise covariance matrix $Q_h$. By keeping $Q_h \ge Q_{h,\min} > 0$, the volatility filter's state error covariance $P_{h}$ is prevented from collapsing. This ensures the filter remains responsive to sudden shifts in system volatility.Measurement Noise Saturation: To prevent extreme outliers from driving the feedback loop to diverge, the exponentiated variance must be passed through a saturation block before updating the primary filter :$$R_t = \max\left(R_{\min}, \min\left(R_{\max}, \exp\left(\hat{h}_{t\mid t-1}\right)\right)\right)$$This caps the maximum and minimum Kalman gain, preserving the filter's stability under extreme conditions.Taxonomy of Skill Acquisition and Empirical Learning CurvesIn cognitive engineering and human-system integration, tracking an agent's performance requires modeling how skills are acquired with practice. Skill acquisition typically exhibits a non-linear trajectory, characterized by rapid initial improvement followed by a gradual plateau as the learner approaches an asymptotic limit of mastery.Survey of Empirical Learning Curve ModelsPower Law of PracticeThe classical Power Law of Practice, popularized by Newell and Rosenbloom , expresses performance (typically response time $T$ or error rate) on the $N$-th trial as a power function :$$T(N) = A + B(N + E)^{-\beta}$$where $A$ represents the asymptotic performance floor (the irreducible physical and neural limit of the task) , $B$ is the potential improvement range , $E$ is the learner's prior experience before experimental tracking , and $\beta$ is the learning rate.The defining characteristic of the power law is that its relative learning rate (RLR)—defined as the negative rate of change of expected performance divided by the amount of skill left to be learned—is a hyperbolically decreasing function of practice trials :$$\text{RLR}_{\text{Power}} = -\frac{1}{T(N) - A} \frac{d T(N)}{dN} = \frac{\beta}{N + E}$$This hyperbolic decay implies that an internal mechanism is actively slowing the learning process over time. Historically, this slowing has been explained by cognitive chunking theories, where learners build increasingly larger, more complex procedural chunks at a constant rate, but the hierarchical structure of the task limits the availability of higher-level chunks.Exponential-to-Asymptote ModelThe exponential model assumes that the rate of performance improvement is directly proportional to the amount of skill remaining to be learned :$$T(N) = A + B e^{-\alpha N}$$where $\alpha$ is a constant learning rate. The relative learning rate of the exponential model remains constant across all levels of practice :$$\text{RLR}_{\text{Exponential}} = \alpha$$In a landmark meta-analysis, Heathcote, Brown, and Mewhort (2000) demonstrated that the power law is often an artifact of linear aggregation. When individual learning curves—which are fundamentally exponential—are averaged across subjects with heterogeneous learning rates, the aggregated curve mathematically mimics a power function. This aggregation bias has been widely confirmed, suggesting that individual learning is governed by exponential, rather than power, dynamics.APEX Practice FunctionTo bridge the power and exponential formulations, Heathcote, Brown, and Mewhort introduced the APEX practice function. The APEX model generalizes the exponential function by adding a parameter $\beta'$ that allows the relative learning rate to decrease early in practice before stabilizing to a constant rate asymptotically :$$T(N) = A + B (N+1)^{-\beta'} e^{-\alpha N}$$To analyze its learning dynamics, the relative learning rate of the APEX function is derived as:$$\text{RLR}_{\text{APEX}} = -\frac{1}{T(N) - A} \frac{d T(N)}{dN} = \alpha + \frac{\beta'}{N+1}$$This RLR formulation reveals how the APEX function unifies both classical families. Early in practice (small $N$), the relative learning rate is dominated by the hyperbolic term $\beta'/(N+1)$, capturing rapid, power-law-like cognitive adaptation as the learner discovers efficient task strategies. As practice accumulates ($N \to \infty$), the relative learning rate converges to the constant non-zero asymptote $\alpha$ :$$\lim_{N\to\infty} \text{RLR}_{\text{APEX}} = \alpha > 0$$This long-term constant rate represents procedural consolidation. When $\beta' = 0$, the APEX model simplifies to the standard three-parameter exponential.State-Space Encoding of Non-Linear Learning CurvesTo track skill acquisition online, these empirical models must be encoded within a recursive state-space framework. This can be achieved using either a linear state-space model with trend damping or a non-linear parameter-estimation state-space model.Option A: Linear Damped Local Linear Trend ModelThe "fast improvement then plateau" dynamic can be approximated within a strictly linear state-space framework using Holt's damped local linear trend model, formulated under a Single Source of Error (SSOE) specification as an ETS($A, A_d, N$) model. Let the state vector be $x_t = [l_t, b_t]^T$, where $l_t$ represents the current performance level and $b_t$ is the local rate of improvement (trend). The state transition and measurement equations are formulated as :$$y_t = l_{t-1} + \phi b_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$$$$l_t = l_{t-1} + \phi b_{t-1} + \alpha \epsilon_t$$$$b_t = \phi b_{t-1} + \beta \epsilon_t$$where $\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is the single white-noise innovation driving the system , and $\alpha, \beta$ are the smoothing parameters for the level and trend, respectively.The parameter $\phi \in (0, 1)$ acts as the autoregressive trend damping factor. If $\phi \approx 1$, the model updates with a persistent linear trend. If $\phi < 1$, the trend of improvement is damped at each step, forcing the multi-step-ahead forecasts to converge asymptotically to a flat ceiling :$$\hat{y}_{t+h\mid t} = l_t + b_t \sum_{i=1}^h \phi^i \xrightarrow{h\to\infty} l_t + b_t \frac{\phi}{1-\phi}$$This linear state-space formulation is highly robust, computationally trivial, and easily estimated using standard linear Kalman filters.Option B: Non-Linear Parameter-Estimation via EKF/UKFWhen the explicit, invariant cognitive parameters of the learning curve must be isolated, a linear approximation is insufficient. The parameters of the exponential-to-asymptote model must be estimated directly by embedding them within a non-linear state vector :$$\theta_t = \begin{bmatrix} A_t \\ B_t \\ \alpha_t \end{bmatrix}$$Because these parameters represent stationary cognitive traits, their transition dynamics are modeled as a random walk driven by minimal process noise to allow for slow drift or adaptation :$$\theta_t = \theta_{t-1} + w_{\theta,t}, \quad w_{\theta,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q_\theta)$$The measurement equation is highly non-linear with respect to the state vector $\theta_t$ :$$y_t = h(\theta_t, t) + v_t = A_t + B_t e^{-\alpha_t t} + v_t$$To recursively solve this non-linear estimation problem, two main filters are employed:Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)The EKF linearizes the non-linear measurement function $h(\theta_t, t)$ at each step using a first-order Taylor series expansion about the current predicted state $\hat{\theta}_{t\mid t-1}$. The time-varying measurement Jacobian matrix $H_t$ is computed analytically :$$H_t = \left. \frac{\partial h(\theta, t)}{\partial \theta} \right|_{\hat{\theta}_{t\mid t-1}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & e^{-\hat{\alpha}_t t} & -\hat{B}_t t e^{-\hat{\alpha}_t t} \end{bmatrix}$$While the EKF is computationally efficient, its first-order linearization can introduce large errors. If the initial parameter guesses are poor, or if the system undergoes rapid transitions, the linear approximation breaks down, leading to filter divergence.Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)The UKF addresses these linearization errors through a deterministic sampling approach. It represents the mean and covariance of the state using a minimal set of carefully chosen sample points, called sigma points. For a state vector of dimension $n_x = 3$, the UKF selects $2n_x + 1 = 7$ sigma points $\mathcal{X}^{(i)}$ symmetrically around the mean :$$\mathcal{X}^{(0)} = \hat{\theta}$$$$\mathcal{X}^{(i)} = \hat{\theta} + \left[ \sqrt{(n_x + \lambda) P_\theta} \right]_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, n_x$$$$\mathcal{X}^{(i+n_x)} = \hat{\theta} - \left[ \sqrt{(n_x + \lambda) P_\theta} \right]_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, n_x$$where the scaling parameter $\lambda = \alpha_u^2(n_x + \kappa) - n_x$ is controlled by the design parameters $\alpha_u$ and $\kappa$. These sigma points are propagated directly through the non-linear measurement function :$$\mathcal{Y}^{(i)}_t = \mathcal{X}^{(i)}_{A,t} + \mathcal{X}^{(i)}_{B,t} e^{-\mathcal{X}^{(i)}_{\alpha,t} t}$$The predicted measurement mean $\hat{y}_t$ and covariance are then reconstructed by taking a weighted sum of the propagated points. It is computationally trivial, globally stable, and requires no initial parameter tuning, making it highly effective for online adaptive systems and forecasting.When Non-Linear Kalman Filtering (EKF/UKF) is Worth the ComplexityNon-linear parameter estimation via EKF or UKF is necessary when the primary objective is to explicitly isolate the physical and cognitive limits of the learner, such as separating the cognitive learning rate $\alpha$ from the physical motor floor $A$. This parameter extraction is crucial for individual diagnostic profiling and targeted human-in-the-loop interventions. Due to the severe collinearity between the asymptote $A$ and the learning range $B$ in the early stages of learning , the EKF is highly susceptible to divergence. In this context, the UKF's second-order accuracy and numerical stability are well worth the moderate increase in computational complexity.When Particle Filtering is Worth the ComplexityWhile the EKF and UKF are effective for smooth, continuous learning processes, human skill acquisition is often marked by abrupt strategy shifts. For example, when performing cognitive or arithmetic tasks, a learner does not improve in a perfectly uniform manner. Instead, they transition through a sequence of discrete strategy states, such as shifting from slow, multi-step calculation algorithms to direct memory retrieval.These sudden qualitative transitions introduce sharp discontinuities and create a bimodal probability distribution, where the learner's performance splits between the algorithmic state and the retrieval state. Because the EKF and UKF enforce a unimodal Gaussian assumption, they are mathematically incapable of representing this multi-modality. They will smooth over the transition, lag behind the jump, or diverge entirely.In this scenario, a Particle Filter is highly valuable. By propagating a swarm of weighted particles, the Particle Filter can represent the full, non-Gaussian posterior distribution. This allows the filter to track both strategy hypotheses simultaneously, accurately capturing the abrupt strategy shift without loss of tracking stability.
